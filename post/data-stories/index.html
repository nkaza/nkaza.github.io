Telling Stories with Data

Last updated on Aug 6, 2025 8 min read teaching

We communicate by telling stories. In our professional world, we often use data to support our stories. But how do we tell compelling stories with data? This post explores, very briefly, the art and science of data storytelling, providing insights and techniques to help you craft narratives that resonate with your audience.

An Exercise

Because I am quirky (a polite way of putting at it), I have been collecting information about whenever gasoline is added to my car. Take about 30 minutes to explore this data, in whatever software and paint a picture of me.

Here is what Microsoft Copilot ‘thought’ the story is. Can you do better?

Here’s a data-driven report on seasonal variations in fuel efficiency, with outliers removed for clarity:

This report analyzes fuel efficiency trends across seasons using cleaned gas consumption data. Outliers were excluded using the interquartile range (IQR) method to ensure accuracy and reliability.

Average MPG by Season

  • Winter: 29.34 MPG
  • Spring: 29.83 MPG
  • Summer: 30.45 MPG
  • Fall: 29.45 MPG

Insights

  1. Summer leads in efficiency: With an average of 30.45 MPG, summer months consistently showed the highest fuel efficiency. This is likely due to favorable driving conditions—dry roads, fewer cold starts, and more highway travel during vacations.

  2. Spring shows improvement: Averaging 29.83 MPG, spring reflects a transition from winter’s inefficiencies. Warmer temperatures and smoother driving conditions contribute to better performance.

  3. Fall remains steady: At 29.45 MPG, fall maintains solid efficiency, though slightly lower than spring and summer. This may be due to cooling temperatures and a return to routine commuting.

  4. Winter lags behind: With the lowest average of 29.34 MPG, winter’s cold weather, increased idling, and potentially hazardous road conditions reduce fuel economy.

Conclusion

Seasonal variations in MPG are modest but measurable. The data suggests that environmental factors—temperature, road conditions, and driving patterns—play a significant role in fuel efficiency. For vehicle owners, this highlights the importance of seasonal maintenance and adapting driving habits to optimize fuel usage.

The Elements of a Good Data Story

There are no rules for good writing, but there are some effective practise and guidelines. Consider these, use them with judgement and break them when you need to.

Who, What and Why

Audience

Good story starts with identifying the audience. Who are they? What do they care about? What is their level of expertise and pre conceptions? Tailoring the story to the audience is crucial.

The story you tell about your work to your faculty advisor will differ from the version you share with policy makers, journalists, or industry professionals. This isn’t about changing your findings– It is about emphasizing different aspects for different purposes. There is no ‘right’ story, only a (maybe two) right story for a particular audience, at that time, with this purpose.

Transformation

The most compelling stories show transformation: How did our/your understanding change? What problem was solved? What new questions emerged? If your research simply confirms what everyone already ‘knew’ (justified beliefs) without revealing new insights or implications, you don’t yet have a story.

Purpose

A good data story has a clear purpose. What is the purpose of your story? Who are you trying to convince? What feelings are you hoping to evoke? What actions do you want your audience to take? (e.g. give you resources). What understanding are you hoping to generate (e.g. Aesop’s fables)? How do you want to complicate these understandings?

Should the audience be inspired? Should they be convinced of your abilities? Should they feel sad/outraged?

A story without a clearly articulated purpose (e.g. Kerouac’s On the Road) might make a good reading, but that is not what we are after here.

The Process

Data Exploration

Spend a lot of time understanding the data generating processes and the data itself. Look for patterns, trends, and anomalies. Use visualizations to help you see the data in different ways. Most of these you will have to discard, but they will help you understand the data better.

Before jumping into the deep end of data exploration, think about the context of data collection. Much of the context to the data is not in the data itself, but it might contain some clues. Otherwise, you will have to find out or figure out the metadata. Think about how the data is collected. Think about what you think is being measured and if it is indeed what is measured. Think about the measuring instruments and their biases. Think about the context in which the data was collected. Think about the people who used these instruments. How well do you think the instruments are calibrated? Think about the people who structured the data once it is collected. Think about the time spans. Think about units, scales and other mundane things that are so often overlooked and so often crucial.

All data is a representation, abstraction and simplification of the world. As such, the world is interpreted, codified, classified, and reduced by many people, things and processes along the way. You need to have a healthy scepticism about what you are measuring and what you are analysing and what the world is.

Depth

A good story is not about the exhaustive scope. You will need depth. You will need a thesis to anchor your story. One way to think about it is to identify 4-5 core findings from your research that you consider most significant. These might be:

  • Surprising results that contradicted expectations
  • Patterns that emerged across different analyses
  • Methodological innovations you developed
  • Theoretical frameworks you developed or tested or refined
  • Applications of your work

For each finding, identify the “moments of change”:

  • What did we think before this research?
  • What changed in our understanding?
  • What new questions emerged?
  • What became possible that wasn’t before?

You may need to keep exploring, editing and discarding, if you ask yourself, “How did things change because of this research?” If nothing changed you need to discard it.

Settle on 1-2 core findings that you think are most significant. These will be the backbone of your story.

The Presentation

Your audience needs to understand what is at stake. Thus your presentation will be different for different audiences. You need to set the stage for them. You need to help them understand why they should care. This could be using motivating example. This could be identifying a overlooked concept in the literature. This could be a newspaper or a prior article that triggered a question. You need to help them understand the context of your work. You need to help them understand the stakes.

Structure

Every story has a beginning, middle and end. The middle is probably interesting only to a select few, who are in your field. Much of graduate education is focused on this middle (research design, research methods etc.) However, the most compelling things in a story for your audience are the beginning and the end. The beginning is where you set the stage, identify the problem, and hook your audience. The end is where you summarize your key points, discuss implications, and suggest next steps.

Exhibits

Just because you are able to produce a pretty visualisation, does not mean you should use it. Every exhibit (table, figure, chart, map) should have a purpose. It should help your audience understand something they could not understand otherwise. It should be easy to read and interpret. It should be labelled clearly and should be integrated into the narrative. It is important to remember that the exhibits are not faithful representation of the data. They are crucial evidence supporting the argument you are making. They should be used judiciously and in the right spots.

Language

The audience condition your language. If you are presenting to your TikTok audiences, you better use memes and dance moves. Master your audience’s language, not yours. If you are writing for an academic audience, you can use some tips that I wrote down a couple of decades ago. Some of them might even be relevant or in vogue.

Editing, Presenting, Reworking

A good story is refined over time. It is transformed over many iterations and many edits. It is reworked based on feedback. Writing is part of the analyses rather than what comes after it. It is only through writing and rewriting that we clarify our thoughts, go on tangents, find unexpected connections, and discover the wrongness of our deeply held beliefs.

You should be ruthless in your editing. You should be willing to discard things that do not serve the story. You should be willing to rework things that do not work.

Conclusion

Telling stories requires a great deal of practise. It is a skill that is acquired through repetition and reflection, honed through feedback and critique. It is developed through reading and listening to good stories and interrogating them. My fervent hope is that you will use this course to develop those skills. I also hope that you will be able to tell stories that matter, stories that change minds, stories that inspire action, stories that make a difference.
